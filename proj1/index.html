Task 1

Rasterizing triangles is done by placing colored pixels on the screen by sampling from a binary indicator function that determines whether a pixel is inside the triangle or not given the three vertices of the triangle.

In order to do this, we must first determine whether or not each pixel should be included in the triangle or not. This is done by making sure a given point is always on the left side of an edge, as you follow the edges of the triangle counter-clockwise:

A normal vector is created for each side of the triangle, and as long as the dot product between the normal and the test point vector is non-negative, the point is inside the triangle.

In our implementation, instead of forcing the order of the triangle’s points to be in a counter-clockwise ordering, we calculate the normal vector for each side in an arbitrary order, and flip the sign of the line test depending on whether or not the unused triangle point also passes or fails.

It would be computationally expensive to check every single pixel in the scene for whether it is in a given triangle. In our implementation, we create a tight bounding box around the triangle, ranging between the minimum x position of all the points to the maximum x position of all the points, and similarly for the y coordinates.

Task 2

The super-sampling algorithm samples the scene at a higher frequency, then averages them out into the final framebuffer. This helps to alleviate some of the issues caused by sampling at too low of a frequency, such as jaggies and discontiguous pixels.

Our implementation works by storing the intermediate super-sampled scene in a separate buffer, the sample_buffer. This buffer is sample_rate times larger than the framebuffer, and is then reduced down through averaging to place the samples into the framebuffer.

For each pixel, instead of only sampling (x+0.5,y+0.5)(x + 0.5, y + 0.5)(x+0.5,y+0.5)﻿, we sample sample_rate times around the pixel.

This was implemented through the addition of two for-loops in the triangle rastering methods: for every pixel location, we varied sqrt(sample_rate) times in the x direction, and sqrt(sample_rate) times in the y direction, creating sample_rate unique sample locations within a pixel. The result of each of these samplings was stored in the larger sample_buffer.

Without supersampling, high frequency detail can be completely lost due to sampling rate that is too low. This is especially apparent with the edges of skinny triangles, where some area might not be picked up with a sample_rate of 1 pixel. With supersampling, high frequency information can still be recovered, and noise is decreased through the use of a low-pass filter— the averaging of the sample_buffer into the framebuffer. This results in an anti-aliasing effect.

With a sample_rate=1, the triangle is jaggy and discontiguous, as the triangle’s thin edge is too small to be picked up by that low of a sampling rate. As the sampling rate increases, the triangle becomes much smoother, and results in a more visually accurate rendering as more information is being captured and smoothed.

After creating a corresponding point in a texture from barycentric coordinates, it may not exactly land on a specific texel location. To solve this, we implement two pixel sampling algorithms: nearest neighbor and bilinear sampling Pixel sampling algorithms that convert a float location to a sampled color, given a mapping from integer locations to pixel colors (textures).

Nearest Pixel Sampling

For nearest pixel sampling, each texel coordinate is computed by flooring the sample coordinates to an integer value. This is then used to index into the texture.

Relative Differences

There will be a large difference between the two methods when there is high frequency information or a large variance in textures between texels. In these instances, nearest neighbor sampling will discretize to the nearest texel which may result in rasterized images with jaggy artifacts around edges and other high variance boundaries.

Bilinear interpolation attempts to deal with this by interpolating colors smoothly for samples between texels, which results in much cleaner images with fewer visual artifacts. Bilinear sampling provides this benefit at the cost of performance since the 4 nearest neighbors for a sample must be found and the respective texels must be linearly interpolated, whereas nearest neighbor sampling only needs find the texel closest to the sample which can be accomplished trivially with a floor operator.

Task 6

Level sampling is the process of using different levels of a mipmap, where the lowest level of the mipmap encodes the highest resolution representation of the texture and the highest level encodes the lowest frequency, lowest resolution version of the texture. By computing the norm of the gradient between neighboring texels, we can compute the mipmap level by taking the logarithm of this value. Intuitively, if the log gradient norm is very large, that indicates a higher frequency change between neighboring texels which can cause aliasing. To prevent this, we select a higher mipmap level which encodes lower frequency signals and smooths out the aliasing caused by the high frequency change.

We implemented this by following the above process for each triangle and using pixel sampling (nearest neighbor, bilinear) as described above to compute the appropriate texel color per pixel in the rasterized triangle.

Relative Differences

Between (L_ZERO, P_NEAREST) and (L_ZERO, P_LINEAR), there is a slight smoothing effect. This is because a lot of the visual detail (high frequency information) is lost when choosing only the nearest pixel. With P_LINEAR, the nose bridge of Prof. Sahai is more smoothed, and the glasses frame is more connected, as more information from the high frequency information can be recovered and anti-aliased away.

Between L_ZERO and L_NEAREST, there is a drastic difference. This is because with L_ZERO, we are sampling fairly sparsely in the texel space. This results in more noise, as samples are far apart, resulting in aliasing. With L_NEAREST, this is avoided by selecting a mipmap level that more corresponds with the density that is being sampled.

Between (L_NEAREST, P_NEAREST) and (L_NEAREST, P_LINEAR), there is some additional smoothing. This can be seen notably in Prof. Sahai’s upper eyelid, where there is a significant decrease in the block-like artifacts.
